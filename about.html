What is a webring?

Before social media algorithms decided what you read, the web organized discovery differently.

A webring was a group of sites linked in a loop — each site pointed to the next and previous, and clicking through took you around a community of people making things about the same thing. You could find writing you never would have searched for. Discovery felt human.

Webrings mostly disappeared. A few people have been bringing them back.

Why debate?

Competitive debate produces a specific kind of person: someone who has spent a lot of time thinking carefully about ideas, building arguments, and listening to the other side. Debaters come from everywhere — Nairobi, Oslo, São Paulo, Taipei, everywhere — competing in British Parliamentary, World Schools, policy, Lincoln-Douglas, and every other format.

A lot of them create things. Blogs, newsletters, YouTube channels, podcasts. Writing about rounds, about theory, about the ideas that come up in debate and stay with them afterward.

But they're scattered. Hard to find unless you already know where to look. This ring exists to make the community visible — to connect people across formats, circuits, and countries, and make it a little easier to stumble into something worth reading.

What makes this one different?

Most webrings

Require submitting a pull request on GitHub to join. Only accept traditional websites.

This one

Uses a form. Accepts any platform — websites, Substacks, YouTube channels, podcasts, newsletters.

The GitHub pull request model works well for tech communities — it's what they use every day. But it's a real barrier if you don't write code. And most of the people in this ring aren't here because they code.

Similarly: if you make a weekly newsletter about debate theory, or run a YouTube channel breaking down rounds, that belongs here just as much as a personal website. The format doesn't matter. The content does.
